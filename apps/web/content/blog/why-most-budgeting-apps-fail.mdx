The average budgeting app gets abandoned within 14 days. Not because people don't care about money — they care deeply. But because most apps are designed around a flawed assumption: that people want to budget.

They don't. People want to *know if they're okay*. That's a very different problem, and solving it requires very different design.

## Where Most Apps Go Wrong

**Too much setup.** Before you can use most budgeting apps, you need to create categories, set monthly limits, link bank accounts, configure notifications, and sometimes even classify your existing transactions. That's an hour of work before you've tracked a single peso. Most people quit during setup.

**Category paralysis.** Is a Grab to the grocery store "transport" or "groceries"? Is coffee with a friend "food" or "social"? Every transaction becomes a micro-decision about classification. Multiply that by 5-10 transactions per day and the mental load is exhausting.

**Guilt-driven design.** Red numbers. Warning banners. "You've exceeded your food budget!" Most apps are designed to make you feel bad when you overspend. But guilt doesn't change behavior — it causes avoidance. People close the app to escape the judgment.

**Monthly thinking.** A monthly budget of ₱8,000 for "variable expenses" tells you almost nothing useful on a Tuesday afternoon when you're deciding whether to order food delivery. The number is too abstract to inform daily decisions.

## What We Do Differently

When we built ledgr, we started with one question: what would make someone open a budgeting app every day — not out of obligation, but because it's genuinely useful?

**One number, zero categories.** Your daily allowance is the only number that matters. No categories to set up, no classification decisions, no budget limits to configure. Log what you spend and check your number. Done.

**30-second setup.** Enter your income. Subtract your bills. See your daily allowance. You can be tracking within a minute of signing up. Every barrier we removed is a user who didn't quit during onboarding.

**[Auto-rebalance](/blog/how-auto-rebalance-works) instead of guilt.** Overspend today? Tomorrow's number adjusts. No red banners, no warnings, no shame. Just a new number and a fresh start. The math is honest without being punitive.

**Daily, not monthly.** Your allowance updates every day based on actual spending. It's relevant right now, not at the end of the month when it's too late to change anything.

**[Smart Input](/blog/type-it-like-you-say-it) for zero friction.** Type "coffee 120" and you're done. No forms, no dropdowns, no category selection. Three seconds per transaction means you actually do it consistently.

## The Retention Problem

The real metric for a budgeting app isn't downloads — it's day-30 retention. How many people are still using it after a month?

Most budgeting apps see retention drop below 10% by day 30. That's not a user problem. That's a design problem. If 90% of your users leave, your product is too hard, too guilt-driven, or too irrelevant to daily life.

We're building ledgr around the opposite bet: make the core action (logging expenses) take 3 seconds, make the core insight (your daily number) immediately useful, and remove everything that doesn't serve those two goals.

## Honest Design

We don't promise to make you rich. We don't gamify savings with streaks and badges. We don't pretend that an app can solve structural financial problems.

What we do promise is clarity. One number, every day, that answers the only question that matters: *"How much can I spend today?"*

That's it. And we think that's enough.
