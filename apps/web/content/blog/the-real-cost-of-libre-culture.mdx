Your sweldo just came in. Within the hour, someone in the group chat has dropped the magic word: "Libre!"

In Filipino culture, generosity isn't just nice — it's expected. You got promoted? Libre. Birthday? Libre. Sweldo day? Libre ka na dyan. And refusing feels like saying you don't care.

But here's the quiet truth nobody wants to say out loud: libre culture is expensive, and it disproportionately hurts the person who's trying to budget.

## The Hidden Math

Let's say you treat your team to merienda once a pay period. Nothing fancy — a round of milk tea and some siomai from the corner. That's maybe ₱500-800 per session.

Twice a month, that's ₱1,000-1,600. Over a year: **₱12,000-19,200**. On a ₱25,000/month salary, that's roughly 3-4% of your annual income — spent on social obligation, not choice.

Add birthday treats, payday lunches, and the occasional "I'll get this one" and you're looking at a significant chunk of money that never shows up in a formal budget category but slowly, quietly drains your daily allowance.

## It's Not About Being Kuripot

Let's be clear: generosity is beautiful. Treating people you care about is one of the best uses of money. The problem isn't libre itself — it's when libre becomes automatic, expected, and guilt-driven rather than joyful.

There's a difference between "I want to treat my friends today" and "I have to treat everyone because they'll think I'm cheap if I don't."

The first is generosity. The second is financial peer pressure.

## How to Handle It Without Being "That Person"

**Set a libre budget.** Decide in advance how much you're willing to spend on treats per pay period. ₱500? ₱1,000? Whatever you choose, it's intentional — not reactive. When it's spent, it's spent.

**Suggest splitting.** "Let's split this one" is not a crime. In fact, most people respect it. The ones who don't are probably the ones who never libre back anyway.

**Downsize the gesture.** Instead of treating eight people to a restaurant meal, bring a box of donuts to the office. The social gesture is the same — "I'm sharing with you" — but the cost drops from ₱2,000 to ₱300.

**Rotate.** If your friend group regularly eats out, establish a rotation. This month it's your turn, next month it's someone else's. Equal generosity, shared cost.

**Be honest with close friends.** Real friends won't judge you for saying "I'm watching my budget this month." If they do, that's information about the friendship, not about your finances.

## Track It and See

Here's a challenge: for the next month, every time you libre someone, log it. Don't change your behavior — just track it. At the end of the month, look at the total.

If the number surprises you, that's not a reason for guilt. It's a reason for awareness. And awareness is where better decisions start.
