In Filipino money culture, there are two archetypes: the *kuripot* (the tightwad who saves everything and spends nothing) and the *gastador* (the big spender who treats everyone and worries about the bill later).

Everyone has an opinion about which is worse. Kuripot people get called selfish. Gastador people get called irresponsible. Neither label is fair, and neither extreme actually works.

The real goal isn't to be one or the other. It's to find your balance on the spectrum.

## The Kuripot Trap

Being careful with money is smart. Being so careful that you never enjoy it is a different problem.

Signs you might be too far on the kuripot side:

- You feel genuine anxiety spending money on things you can afford
- You say no to experiences you actually want because of the cost
- You keep money "for emergencies" that never get defined
- Friends have stopped inviting you to things because you always say no
- You have savings but no clear purpose for them

The kuripot trap is saving as a reflex rather than a strategy. Money exists to support your life — not the other way around. If your savings don't have a purpose, you're not being disciplined. You're being fearful.

## The Gastador Trap

Generosity is a Filipino supervalue. But generosity without boundaries is self-destruction in slow motion.

Signs you might be too far on the gastador side:

- You feel pressure to say yes to every social expense
- You treat others even when you can't comfortably afford it
- Your bank balance surprises you (in the wrong direction) regularly
- You avoid looking at your accounts because the number stresses you out
- The day before sweldo feels like a countdown to survival

The gastador trap is confusing generosity with identity. You can be a generous, warm, [libre-loving](/blog/the-real-cost-of-libre-culture) person and still have boundaries with money. The two aren't contradictory.

## Finding the Middle

The healthy middle isn't some magical 50/50 split. It's different for everyone and it shifts with life circumstances. But here are some markers:

**You spend on things that matter to you.** Not what social media says matters. Not what your barkada expects. What actually makes your life better. For some people that's travel. For others it's food. For others it's saving for a house. There's no wrong answer — only the honest one.

**You save with purpose.** Not "just in case" forever. Save for specific things: a 1-month emergency buffer, a new laptop, a trip home for Christmas. When saving has a target, it doesn't feel like deprivation — it feels like progress. A [Flex Bucket](/blog/flex-bucket-saving-without-guilt) helps here.

**You can say yes and no comfortably.** Yes to the dinner you want to attend. No to the one you're only going to out of obligation. The ability to choose — without guilt in either direction — is financial maturity.

**You know your number.** Whether you're kuripot or gastador, knowing your daily allowance keeps you grounded. The kuripot sees they have room to spend. The gastador sees when they're pushing limits. Same tool, different insights.

## It's Not Fixed

Where you sit on the spectrum changes. When money is tight, you lean kuripot — and that's appropriate. When a windfall arrives or you get a raise, you can lean gastador — and that's appropriate too.

The mistake is treating your position as permanent identity. "I'm just kuripot" or "I'm just gastador" stops being a description and starts being an excuse.

Better framing: "Right now, I'm leaning [kuripot/gastador] because [reason]. My daily allowance is ₱X and I'm comfortable with how I'm using it."

> You're not a type. You're a person making daily choices. Make them with your eyes open.
