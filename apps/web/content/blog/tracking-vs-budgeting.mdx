"I track my expenses" and "I budget" sound like the same thing. They're not. And confusing the two is one of the reasons people struggle with both.

## What's the Difference?

**Tracking** is looking backward. It answers: "Where did my money go?" You log expenses after they happen. You review patterns. You notice that you spent ₱4,500 on food delivery last month.

**Budgeting** is looking forward. It answers: "Where should my money go?" You allocate amounts before spending. You decide in advance that food gets ₱5,000 this month and transport gets ₱2,000.

Most apps try to do both at once, and end up doing neither well. You're asked to set category budgets (forward-looking) and then log every transaction (backward-looking), and somehow the two are supposed to meet in the middle.

They rarely do.

## Start With Tracking

If you're not doing either right now, start with tracking. Not because budgeting doesn't matter — but because tracking is easier, builds the habit, and gives you data you actually need.

Here's why tracking first works:

**You can't budget what you don't understand.** How much should you allocate for transport? If you've never tracked it, you're guessing. And guessed budgets fail immediately because they're based on what you *think* you spend, not what you *actually* spend.

**Tracking requires no willpower.** You're not trying to change behavior. You're just observing it. Log what you spend, that's it. No "you went over budget" alerts, no shame, no red numbers.

**Patterns emerge naturally.** After two weeks of tracking, you'll know which days are expensive, which categories dominate, and where the leaks are. You didn't need a budget to discover that — you just needed data.

## When to Add Budgeting

Once you've tracked for 2-4 weeks and you understand your patterns, you have a foundation to budget from. Now your allocations are grounded in reality, not wishful thinking.

But even then, we'd argue that [daily allowance budgeting](/blog/what-is-daily-allowance-budgeting) works better than category budgeting. Instead of setting 10 category limits, you set one number: how much can I spend today?

It's budgeting in its simplest, most honest form.
