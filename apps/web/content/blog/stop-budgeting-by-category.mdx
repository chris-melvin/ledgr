It's the 20th of the month. You set a ₱3,000 grocery budget, but you're already at ₱3,200. Your transport budget has ₱800 left, your "eating out" budget is untouched because you've been cooking, and your "miscellaneous" category is a black hole that somehow absorbed ₱1,500.

Sound familiar?

## The Category Trap

Category budgeting sounds logical: assign every peso a purpose, track where it goes, stay within limits. In theory, it's airtight. In practice, it's exhausting.

Here's what actually happens:

- **Real spending doesn't fit neat boxes.** Is a Grab to the grocery store "transport" or "groceries"? Is coffee with a client "food" or "work"?
- **You rob from one category to fund another.** Every overspend triggers a negotiation with yourself about which category to sacrifice.
- **You stop tracking altogether.** The friction of choosing the right category for every transaction becomes a reason to stop logging expenses entirely.

Categories don't fail because you lack discipline. They fail because life is messy and categories are rigid.

## The Alternative: One Pool, Daily Recalculation

Instead of splitting your money into 8 categories, keep it in one pool. Divide by the days remaining. Spend from that.

Overspent today? Tomorrow's number adjusts automatically. No categories to rob, no guilt about busting a budget that was artificial in the first place.

This is [daily allowance budgeting](/blog/what-is-daily-allowance-budgeting), and it works because it bends with your life instead of breaking against it.

## "But I Want to Know Where My Money Goes!"

Fair point. Knowing your spending patterns is valuable. But there's a difference between **tracking** and **restricting**.

You don't need categories to *control* your spending. You need them to *understand* it — after the fact. That's what analytics are for. Log your expenses with a quick note, and review the patterns later. You'll see that you spend more on weekends, or that food delivery is your biggest leak, without needing to set an artificial ₱2,500 cap on "dining" that you'll break by the 15th anyway.

Tracking gives you data. Categories give you guilt. Choose data.

## The One-Week Challenge

Not convinced? Try this: for one week, forget your categories. Just track one number — your daily allowance. Log every expense, but don't sort it into buckets. At the end of the week, look back at what you spent and where.

You'll probably learn more about your habits in that one week than you did in three months of category budgeting.
