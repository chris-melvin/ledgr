Quick: which day of the week do you spend the most money?

If you're like most people, you have a guess — probably Saturday — but you don't really know. And not knowing is expensive, because patterns you can't see are patterns you can't change.

## Your Month at a Glance

ledgr's calendar view shows your spending as a color-coded month. Each day gets a color based on how you did against your daily allowance:

- **Green** — you spent under your allowance
- **Yellow** — you spent close to your allowance
- **Red** — you went over

After just two weeks of tracking, the calendar starts telling a story. And it's usually not the story you expected.

## Patterns You'll Probably Find

Here are the most common patterns we see:

**The weekend spike.** Monday through Thursday: green, green, green, green. Friday: yellow. Saturday: red. Sunday: red. You're disciplined during the work week, then the weekend arrives and spending doubles.

**The sweldo celebration.** Payday lands and the first two days are red — a nice dinner, some online shopping, "I deserve this." Then the rest of the period is a sea of yellow and green as you compensate.

**The mid-month dip.** Spending drops sharply around the 10th-15th as you realize half the money is gone but the month isn't half over. Then it stabilizes — or panics.

**The social day.** Maybe it's not Saturday. Maybe your expensive day is Wednesday because that's when your barkada meets. The calendar reveals the real pattern, not the one you assumed.

## Awareness Changes Behavior

Here's the interesting part: you don't need to *do* anything with this information for it to help. Just seeing the pattern changes your behavior.

When you can see that every Friday is red, you start making slightly different Friday decisions. Not dramatic ones — you don't cancel your plans. Maybe you eat before going out. Maybe you take the jeep instead of Grab. Maybe you suggest the less expensive restaurant.

Small adjustments, driven by real data, not by guilt or arbitrary rules.

## The Two-Week Challenge

The calendar view only gets useful after enough data. Here's the deal: track every expense for 14 days. Don't change your behavior. Don't try to be "good." Just log what you spend, honestly, every day.

After two weeks, open the calendar view. Look at the colors. Find your pattern.

- Which day is consistently red?
- Is there a green streak you didn't expect?
- Does payday trigger a spending spike?
- Are weekdays tighter than you thought?

Once you see it, you can't unsee it. And that's when real change starts — not because an app told you to spend less, but because you can see, clearly, where your money actually goes.
